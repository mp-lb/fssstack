# Positioning Sequence

Follow an April Dunford-style positioning sequence. Keep it strict and document-driven.

## Core Questions

1. What does the product do?
2. What would customers use or do instead?
3. What can this product do that alternatives cannot credibly claim?
4. Why do those differences matter?
5. Who cares most about those differences?
6. What market frame makes the value obvious?
7. What is the concise positioning statement?

## Document Rules

Write one small document per question.

Do not let the positioning statement lead the process. Derive it from:

1. product features
2. alternatives and competitors
3. unique capabilities
4. value drivers
5. ideal customer
6. market context

## Alternatives

Include direct competitors and replacement behaviors.

Common alternatives:

- other SaaS
- spreadsheets
- agencies or consultants
- internal tools
- doing nothing
- manual workflows
- email
- documents
- chat tools
- generic AI tools

Use `list-of-competitors.md` for both named competitors and non-product alternatives.

## Unique Capabilities

Only include capabilities that competitors would struggle to claim honestly.

Reject generic strengths such as:

- easy to use
- saves time
- AI-powered
- all-in-one
- better workflow
- modern interface

Keep asking whether a named alternative could plausibly say the same thing.

## Value Drivers

Translate each unique capability into value by asking "so what?" until the business or workflow consequence is clear.

Good value drivers name:

- pain removed
- risk reduced
- workflow unlocked
- cost avoided
- revenue opportunity
- speed gained
- quality improved
- confidence created

## Ideal Customer

Choose the customer segment that cares most about the value drivers.

Prefer specific situations over broad personas:

- painful workflow
- urgent use case
- high-friction environment
- constrained team
- expensive failure mode
- regulatory or trust pressure
- unusual volume, complexity, or stakes

## Market Context

Choose the category or comparison frame that makes the unique value easiest to understand.

Avoid categories that force the product to compete on weak dimensions.

## Positioning Statement

Use this format:

```text
For [specific user or situation],
who [use or struggle with alternative],
[product] is a [market category]
that [primary value],
unlike [main alternatives],
because [unique capability].
```

## Stress Test

Before finalizing `positioning-statement.md`, check that it is:

- specific
- differentiated
- understandable
- grounded in evidence
- attractive to the ideal customer
- hard for competitors to copy without lying
